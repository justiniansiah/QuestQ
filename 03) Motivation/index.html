Motivation

Queues are a common phenomenon that arise due the fact that resources are limited, and time is required in order to provide some sort of service to make a good available. When this duration of time which shall be denoted as service time becomes too large, customers that are paying to obtain that good or service become dissatisfied as opportunity cost for utilizing that time in better ways becomes increasingly larger. Designing queueing systems involves obtaining a balance between service time to prepare the good from a pool of limited resources and the waiting time customers experience.

Traditionally queues in food and beverage outlets, and queues in Singaporean food courts and hawker centres have followed a First-come-First-serve systems, in order to deliver the product first to customers who made the order first. This sometimes results in large queue lengths and long queue times if the service rate of the store is unable to match the arrival rate of customers. This can be frustrating for both the customers, who need to deal with the time they spend queuing, as well as the store owners, who are pressured to serve the growing queue.

In recent times, there have been an increasing number of shops and stores opting for alternative queueing systems, most notably the split queue system. The first queue follows the traditional FCFS queue, where customers queue to place their order and payment. Afterwards, customers proceed to wait for their food/items in the second queue. Orders may not be served in the sequence they were made. This allows shopkeepers to reduce production time by preparing similar dishes together as they have more knowledge about what the incoming orders are.

Through this project we hope to find out if this alternative queuing system is indeed effective in reducing wait times or is this a perceived effect on the customers' end.
